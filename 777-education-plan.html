Primary Strategies

The Three Pillars

These three strategies are the core of the campaign. Together they create the mechanism, the scale, and the multiplication needed to reach 7 million people.

Foundation  ·  Pillar 1

Train the Teacher,
Reach the Class

300K teachers × 25 = 7.5M students reached through educator training

The most scalable entry point into schools is through the teachers who already have a captive audience every day. Run free one-hour "Boom Buddy for Educators" trainings — in-person or virtual — that certify teachers and school counselors to lead a MoM session with their class. Target wellness teachers, homeroom teachers, and school counselors first, as they already own the mental health curriculum time. One trained educator can guide 25–30 students through the practice every single day. The math does the rest.

Key Actions

  • Develop a concise "Boom Buddy for Educators" training program (1 hour, self-paced online option)
  • Create a dedicated teacher kit: session guide, classroom deck, printable take-home sheet
  • Partner with teacher professional development networks and SEL organizations
  • Prioritize middle and high school wellness / advisory period teachers as first cohort
  • Track completions and student reach through a simple sign-up form

Scale  ·  Pillar 2

School District
Partnerships

1 district sign-on unlocks dozens of schools simultaneously

Rather than approaching individual schools one at a time, go to the decision-maker above them: the district wellness director or superintendent. A single district partnership can activate 20, 50, or 100 schools at once through existing communication channels and staff training days. The pitch should not be about meditation in the abstract — frame it squarely around measurable student outcomes that districts are already mandated to address: focus, anxiety reduction, and Social-Emotional Learning (SEL) benchmarks. Come with data, a simple pilot proposal, and a clear ask.

Key Actions

  • Develop a School District Proposal Deck (adapted from the existing Corporate Presentation Deck)
  • Identify and prioritize the top 50 districts by student population and SEL program presence
  • Frame the proposal around SEL mandate alignment and student wellbeing metrics
  • Offer a structured 7-day pilot with pre/post student feedback
  • Identify Isha volunteers or MoM practitioners already within those districts as internal advocates

Multiplier  ·  Pillar 3

Family Challenge

1 student = family of 4 schools as the gateway to households

Every school is a direct gateway to thousands of families. When a student learns the MoM practice at school and takes it home, the campaign's reach multiplies well beyond the classroom. A simple "7-Day Family Meditation Challenge" take-home — a one-pager or QR code linking to the app — invites the whole household to participate. A family of four that meditates together for 7 days counts as four practitioners, not one. This is the mechanism that bridges school reach to the 7 million target.

Key Actions

  • Design a print-ready "7-Day Family Meditation Challenge" take-home sheet with QR code to the app
  • Create a family-friendly onboarding flow on the app for first-time adult users
  • Offer a class-level or school-level "family participation" tracking option (optional, privacy-safe)
  • Develop a short follow-up at the end of 7 days — a reflection prompt or digital badge
  • Deploy through teacher-trained schools as part of their standard student kit

Supporting Strategies

The Amplifiers

These four strategies work alongside the primary pillars — each one feeding directly into at least one of them, expanding reach through additional channels and audiences.

7-7-7 School Challenge

Feeds: Train the Teacher  Feeds: District Partnerships

A branded, time-bound campaign mechanism that gives schools a concrete, shareable event to rally around. The "7-7-7 School Challenge" — 7 minutes of MoM, every day for 7 days, as a class — is tied directly to World Mental Health Day on October 10. It works because it gives teachers a low-commitment, fully packaged program to run, and gives district wellness directors a campaign they can endorse and promote across schools. The challenge creates urgency, a clear start and end date, and a completion milestone (a shareable certificate for participating classrooms).

  • Build a simple sign-up landing page for schools and teachers
  • Send daily reminder emails or texts to participating teachers
  • Create a shareable classroom completion certificate for social amplification
  • Time the launch 3–4 weeks before October 10 to allow sign-ups to build

Student Ambassador Network

Feeds: Train the Teacher

Peer-led programs consistently outperform adult-led ones in school adoption — students trust and follow other students. Train one student leader per school as a MoM Student Ambassador. They run a short session for their peers, during lunch, advisory period, or an after-school slot. The ambassador model extends the trained-teacher foundation into spaces where a teacher cannot go — student clubs, dorm common rooms, peer support groups — and does it with more cultural credibility. Build a simple ambassador kit so any motivated student can run a session independently.

  • Create a lightweight "Student Ambassador Kit" — session guide, intro deck, FAQ
  • Run a short virtual onboarding session for new ambassadors monthly
  • Recognize ambassadors publicly (certificates, social features) to incentivize participation
  • Target campus MoM clubs and student government wellness committees as first recruits

After-School Program Partnerships

Feeds: District Partnerships

National after-school networks — Boys & Girls Clubs, YMCA youth programs, Title I extended learning programs — serve millions of students daily and are actively seeking wellness content. They have structured daily time slots where a 7-minute practice fits naturally. Unlike working through individual schools, a single national network partnership can unlock thousands of locations simultaneously — making it one of the highest-leverage scale levers available outside of formal district agreements. These programs also serve populations underrepresented in traditional school wellness initiatives.

  • Identify 3–5 national after-school network leads for an initial partnership conversation
  • Adapt the Educator training kit for after-school program staff (not teachers)
  • Propose a 7-day pilot for one network location cluster before broader rollout
  • Frame the pitch around youth mental health programming requirements many networks already have

University Mental Health Week

Parallel Track (University / Young Adults)

Most universities already run a Mental Health Awareness Week in early October, aligned naturally with World Mental Health Day on October 10. This is a ready-made, opt-in audience that requires no calendar creation — the infrastructure is already in place. Partner with campus counseling centers, student government wellness committees, and psychology departments to host a MoM session as part of their official programming. Existing MoM campus clubs and trained student ambassadors serve as on-ground facilitators. This track runs in parallel to the K-12 school strategy and brings in a distinct demographic: college students and young adults.

  • Identify universities with active campus MoM clubs or nearby Isha centers
  • Reach out to campus counseling centers with a co-hosting proposal by late August
  • Provide a turnkey "MoM at Your Campus Mental Health Week" event package
  • Encourage sessions to conclude with the 7-day Family Challenge to extend reach to families
